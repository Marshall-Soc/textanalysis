Introduction
This course has been devoted to introducing you to a wide range of
computational tools and frameworks for analyzing text data from a
social science perspective.
The growing proliferation of digital/digitized texts is, in no small part,
responsible for the growing interest in computational text analysis
among social scientists.
However, the rise in access to digital/digitized texts also introduces
some ethical concerns that we should consider.

Existing Ethical Standards in Social Research
Social scientists aﬃliated with most institutions have some baseline ethical
standards they must meet.
Speciﬁcally, we have to meet Collaborative Institutional Training Initiative
(CITI) standards for carrying out research with human subjects.
An institution’s Institutional Review Board (IRB) is responsible for making
sure all institution-aﬃliated researchers listed on a registered study have
satisﬁed human subjects research training and that the study’s protocol
follows all federal, state, and local regulations.
Here’s a link to Notre Dame’s Human Research webpage.

Digital Curveballs
As Salganik (2018:288-93) notes, the digital age has introduced some new
concerns.
These include:
More power on the part of the data collector/researcher
The “Panopticon”
“Database of ruin” (Ohm 2010; cited in Salganik 2018:290)
Uncertainty on how to use this power
Mass surveillance
“Unanticipated secondary use” (p. 290)
Inconsistent “rules, laws, and norms” (p. 293)

Respect for Persons
Respect individual people as autonomous beings.
People with “diminished autonomy” get extra protections in research
settings.
Pregnant women
Prisoners
Children
Instructor: Marshall A. Taylor SOC/MDSC 43919 April 15, 2019 11 / 21
Beneﬁcence
Maximize beneﬁts, minimize risks.
Two parts (Salganik 2018:296-98)
1
Risk-beneﬁt analysis
2
Decide: do beneﬁts outweigh the risks?
Helpful: IRBs must include a member external to the institution (Salganik
2018:297).
Instructor: Marshall A. Taylor SOC/MDSC 43919 April 15, 2019 12 / 21
Justice
Beneﬁts and risks should be equitably distributed across the population.
No particular group should “bear the costs of research” while another
“reaps its beneﬁts” (Salganik 2018:298).
Two dimensions:
1
Protection
2
Access
Questions surrounding compensation for participating in the research.

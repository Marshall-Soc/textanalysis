What is Sentiment Analysis?
Sentiment analysis is used to determine the emotional valence of a
text.
Most sentiment analyses involve tracking a text’s positivity,
negativity, and/or polarity, where polarity is just a measure of the
extent to which a text is more negative or more positive.
There are also emotion classiﬁcation tools, which help us classify
texts based on their most dominant expressed emotions such as joy,
anger, and fear.

The Dictionaries
The polarity and classiﬁcation analyses we’re going to do today are
dictionary-based.
There are countless sentiment dictionaries out there, each constructed in a
diﬀerent way.
Today we’re going to use Matthew L. Jocker’s (2017) sentiment dictionary
and Saif Mohammad and Peter Turney’s (2013) emotion word dictionary.
Jocker’s dictionary was constructed by have multiple human coders tag
each sentence in a series of novels and then extracting out the key terms
that deﬁned the positivity/negativity of each sentence. Mohammad and
Turney’s dictionary was constructed using Amazon MTurk to crowdsource
the coding of over 14,000 words across 8 emotion categories.

How the Sentiment Dictionary Works
The algorithm behind sentimentr is a little complicated, but at its core it
is trying to ﬁnd the ratio of positive words to negative words per some unit
of analysis—usually sentences (this is why we did not remove punctuation).
Basically, the algorithm using the dictionary to “tag” each word in the
sentence that is in the dictionary. Then, using the words that occur just
before and just after each sentiment term (i.e., it’s context words), and a
set of grammatical rules, the sentiment term is further weighted by
whether or not the context words negate, amplify, or de-amplify the
expressed sentiment (Rinker 2019).
Context words are important for calculating sentiment since they can
change the meaning of the sentiment term: e.g., “not angry” signals the
absence of anger, and “absolutely furious” signals that you are beyond
just being angry.

Once the sentiment terms are identiﬁed and weighted, the polarity score
for each sentence is calculated by summing the terms per sentence and
normalizing this sum by the sentence’s word count. Positive scores
indicate the sentence is more positive; negative scores indicate the
sentence is more negative.

read more about the mechanics behind it all here.

Emotion Classiﬁcation
We are often more interested in quantifying the expression of more speciﬁc
emotions.
The syuzhet package makes this possible with the get nrc sentiment()
function (NRC stands for National Research Council Canada, which
funded the research behind Mohammad and Turney’s dictionary).
The process isn’t as sophisticated as the sentiment polarity analysis: it just
counts the number of words in each document that are in each of the
emotion word dictionaries. So there are no valence modiﬁers, but it’s still
pretty useful.
There are dictionaries for the following 8 emotions: anger, anticipation,
disgust, fear, joy, sadness, surprise, and trust. There are also more general
“positivity” and “negativity” dictionaries, but we’ll ignore those.

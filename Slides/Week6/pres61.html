Introduction
We’ve talked a bit about the idea of “machine learning.”
Machine learning is an interdisciplinary ﬁeld focused on the study
and creation of algorithms that are optimized to perform functions
with little to no human guidance.
The machines take some input of training or instructional
information, “learn” from it, and then execute the speciﬁed function
based on what it learned from this input information.

Supervised vs. Unsupervised Learning
The goal of any machine learning algorithm is to extract previously
unknown patterns in data. The initial input provided by humans—or
how much we “train” the algorithm to ﬁnd those patterns—can vary.
There are two ideal-typical forms of machine learning research. There
is supervised learning and unsupervised learning.
Today we’ll talk about supervised learning, speciﬁcally in the context
of text analysis, and even more speciﬁcally on one type of supervised
learning with texts: document classiﬁcation.
We’ll talk about unsupervised learning on Wednesday.

Supervised Learning
According to Ignatow and Mihalcea, “the task of supervised learning . . .
consists of using an automatic system to learn from a history of
occurrences of a certain ‘event’ and consequently make predictions about
future occurrences of that event” (2018:63).
Supervised learning algorithms “come at the cost of annotated data,
which is required to train the supervised algorithms, but they have
the beneﬁt of scalability, as once trained they can be used to classify
essentially any amount of unlabeled data” (2018:119, emphasis
added).
You can think of a few methods we’ve talked about thus
far—sentence-based sentiment analysis, named-entity recognition, and
part-of-speech tagging—as supervised learning tools. Essentially, if the
method requires training data and testing and/or validation data, you
are dealing with supervised learning.

Document Classiﬁcation
Document classiﬁcation is a type of text-based supervised learning task.
The goal is to take a handful of documents that have already been
classiﬁed as being about (or written by, or produced by, etc.) x, y , or z,
and then train a classiﬁer to determine which documents in another set of
data are about (or written by, produced by, etc.) x, y , or z.

The k Statistic
The confusionMatrix() output provides some other useful information,
one of which is the kappa (k) statistic.
k is a measure that shows how well our predicted categories actually
predict the observed categories. Values closer to 1 indicate strong
prediction; values closer to 0 indicate random chance. So a κ of .241 isn’t
great—but I’ve seen worse.

Better Performance
In an actual application, there are a number of things we could do to make
the classiﬁer perform better:
Have a more “balanced” distribution of category frequencies.
Use more terms to subset the training DTM.
Use a larger training set.
Experiment with diﬀerent classiﬁers, such as support vector machines
and random forest models.

Scaling Up
In my view, supervised document classiﬁcation has some of the greatest
potential for integrating computational text analysis into traditional social
science research.
Why?
Because of its “scalability,” and Ignatow and Mihalcea (2018:119) note.
King, Pan, and Robert’s study on state criticism and online censorship in
China (2013) is one great example of this.
Both qualitative and quantitative researchers alike spend a lot of time
coding their data into categories, themes, etc. Imagine how much
classiﬁcation tasks can help when you have really large samples!

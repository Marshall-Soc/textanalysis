Term Frequency Weighting
So far, we have been working with a DTM with term frequency
weightings. You can check this by highlighting the DTM name—in this
case, ny.dtm—and running it. Notice that the “weighting” is set to “term
frequency (tf).”
It is “weighted” in the sense that cell values can take on values other than
0 and 1. If the cells were all 0 or 1, we would call this a “binary matrix,”
since only 2 values are possible. A binary document-term matrix might use
0s to represent that a term does not ever appear in a document, and 1s to
represent that a term appears at least once in a document.
So in this case, each cell in our document-term matrix is “weighted” by the
number of times that a particular term appears in a particular document.

An Alternate Weighting
But using term frequencies to weight a DTM can be problematic.
For example, what if one word is overrepresented in one document? For
example, “white” occurs 10 times in our 32 document corpus, but what if
just one (or a select few) documents account for all 10 occurrences?
One popular alternate weighting factor allows us to see the terms that
occur a lot in a particular document but not very often in the corpus as a
whole. These are called tf-idf scores, or term frequency - inverse
document frequency scores.
tf-idf scores help us identify which terms are most unique to particular
documents.

tf-idf scores
A word’s tf-idf score in a particular document is the ratio of two numbers:
it’s frequency in that document (tf), and the inverse of the number of
documents within the corpus that feature that word (idf).

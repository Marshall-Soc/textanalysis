What is a corpus?
A term from linguistics, where there is a sub-ﬁeld called corpus linguistics.
Corpus linguistics is the study of written and/or spoken language patterns
in structured—and oftentimes large—collections of naturally-produced
texts.
A single collection of texts is called a corpus. The plural is corpora.
So, a corpus is just your text dataset.

Corpora in R
The primary package that we will be using to import and clean up our
documents in preparation for analysis is the tm package—short for “text
mining” (Feinerer 2018a).
The tm package ﬁrst reads in your data and arranges it into a corpus.
In other words, it takes the input and tells R to recognize them as a
collection of text documents.

Preprocessing the Texts
Preprocessing is the term text analysts use to refer to the steps they take
to make standardize the corpus in some way and make it “analyzeable”
using computational or quantitative methods. It’s kind of like data analysis
before the real data analysis—thus the pre in preprocessing.
Some common preprocessing steps include:
Removing punctuation
Removing capitalization
Removing numbers
Removing stop words
Stemming the documents
Removing excess whitespace
We’ll address each in turn.

Stop Words
In text analysis, a stop word is a commonly-used word that is not
considered to carry much in the way of semantic meaning.
What qualiﬁes as a stop word largely depends on the research context, but
common English stop words include article adjectives (“the,” “an,” and
“a”) and conjunctions (“but,” “for,” and “but”).
We often want to remove these sorts of words because they are incredibly
common and vary little in how they are used across documents. They
typically serve more of a syntactical function than a semantic one.

Word Stemming
We often want to stem our documents as part of the preprocessing stage.
We do this when we want diﬀerent manifestations of the same word to be
recognized as such. For instance, if want “run,” “runs,” and “running” to
be recognizes as the same word, we reduce them to a “common base
form” (Manning et al. 2009:32): run.

Word Stemming
The stemming algorithm we’re going to use—the Porter stemming
algorithm (Porter 1980)—uses a series of suﬃx removal rules to hone in
on the following:
Unstemmed Documents, Post Some Preprocessing, Focus on Suﬃxes
Document #1: running ran
Document #2: run gone many runs
It then outputs the word stems so that all versions of “run” will be seen as
one entity:
Stemmed Documents
Document #1: run ran
Document #2: run gone mani run

Word Stemming
You probably noticed two things:
“ran” wasn’t stemmed to “run”
“many” was stemmed to “mani”
To the ﬁrst point, word stemming isn’t always great for lumping together
past tense verbs with their present tense counterparts. In such cases you
either want to substitute in the present verb form before stemming, or use
a more sophisticated process known as lemmatization which reduces
words to the “base or dictionary form of a word” (Manning et al. 2009:32).

The Document-Term Matrix
Now that we have our corpus preprocessed to the extent that we want, we
are ready to convert it to a document-term matrix.
A document-term matrix is simply a matrix with documents as rows, words
as columns, and cells some indication of the extent to which a particular
word is used in a particular document. The most common cell entry is the
term frequency, but we’ll talk about some alternatives on Wednesday.
For a lot of text analysis methods, the document-term matrix—or
DTM—is the standard input. In other words, it is the object around which
many of the tools we’ll talk about in here revolve.

Sparsity
There’s one ﬁnal preprocessing option that you should consider after
you’ve created your DTM: What to do with very infrequent words?
You can remove very infrequent words using the removeSparseTerms()
function.

In the code below, setting the sparsity level to .95 tells the function that
you want to remove all words that fail to show up in at least 95% of the
corpus documents. This a higher bar for removal than a sparsity level of
.1, which would indicate that you want to remove terms that fail to show
